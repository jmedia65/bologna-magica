Tarot study

The first thing is to get your own tarot deck if you don't have one already. There are many available and choice is really a personal one - what works for you? My journey started with Waite-Smith, then Paul Case's B.O.T.A. version, then Crowley and Harris's epic deck. These days I've gone back to basics with the Marseille tarot - specifically the late Jean-Claude Flornoy's lovely restoration of the tarot of Jean Dodal - you can get it from his website Tarot History.

I suggest that you start by simply looking at each card. Take your time. Consider the posture and gestures of the figures in the trumps and court cards, and the arrangement and decorations of the symbols in the cards of the four suits. Lay the cards out in rows and columns, by suits and by numbers, and look for relationships. Allow each image to become a part of you, to live in your consciousness.

There's a huge amount of literature about the tarot, from its history to its use for divination to its use for ritual magic. All of these can be interesting and valuable depending upon where you are on your journey. I've put together a short list of the books that I have found useful here.
